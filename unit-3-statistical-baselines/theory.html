The "how is MSTL actually computed?" slide Ben asked for — the mechanics behind the previous diagnostic. STL is an ITERATIVE pair of loess (locally-weighted regression) smooths: estimate the trend by smoothing the series with the seasonal part removed, then estimate the seasonal component by smoothing each cycle-subseries (all the 8am values together, all the 8:15 values, …), subtract, re-estimate the trend, loop until it stabilises. MSTL just runs that machinery once PER period so it can pull out a daily AND a weekly season at once — the figure's right column. The load-bearing framing (Ben): there are TWO routes to handle seasonality — (a) seasonal differencing INSIDE SARIMA (the in-model route, the (P,D,Q)_m piece, next-but-one beat), and (b) STL/MSTL-decompose then model the remainder (the decomposition route). In THIS deck MSTL is the DIAGNOSTIC that reveals the structure; differencing is what actually makes the series stationary for ARIMA. Anticipated question: "do I have to pick one?" — no: many workflows decompose to SEE the structure, then difference to MODEL it. Next: the differencing math that does the in-model route.

The fix that satisfies the stationarity property — still MODEL-AGNOSTIC (Ben's reframe: do NOT name ARIMA here; differencing is a transformation of the SERIES). Two distinct knobs: the regular difference d removes a trend, the SEASONAL difference D removes the periodic cycle. Emphasise differencing is REVERSIBLE — you model the differenced series then 'integrate' back to speed (this foreshadows the 'I' in ARIMA, Beat 3, where we'll explicitly call back to this slide). The "write the differencing math out" slide (Ben Change 1). The backshift operator B is the bookkeeping that makes the rest of Beat 3 readable: B nudges every index back one step, B^m back one season. A REGULAR difference (1−B) subtracts the previous value and removes a trend; a SEASONAL difference (1−B^m) subtracts the same point one cycle ago and removes the cycle. Read the figure left to right: B shifts the curve back one step; (1−B) flattens a rising trend; (1−B^m) flattens a repeating cycle. The load-bearing traffic fact (Williams & Hoel): combining ONE regular and ONE seasonal difference, (1−B)(1−B^m)y_t, is the move that usually delivers weak stationarity for traffic. CHOOSING the orders comes next slide. This B-notation is exactly what the 'I' and the SARIMA polynomial forms use in Beat 3 — so this slide is the algebra the model slides lean on. Anticipated question: "why an operator, not just a formula?" — because it composes: (1−B)(1−B^m) expands cleanly, and the AR/MA polynomials are written the same way.

The "how many times do I difference?" slide — and the honest warning. d (the regular order) comes from a unit-root test: run ADF, and if it still fails, difference again; for traffic d=1 almost always suffices because speed mean-reverts. D (the seasonal order) comes from how strong the seasonal component is (seasonal-strength heuristics in fpp3); for a clear daily cycle D=1. The TRAP Ben wants named: OVER-differencing. If you difference more than the series needs you INFLATE the variance (you're subtracting two noisy numbers) and you INDUCE spurious moving-average structure (an artefact MA term the data didn't have) — so the discipline is "difference the LEAST amount that makes it stationary," not "difference until it looks flat." Reuse the differencing figure so the "raw wanders → differenced is flat" picture is fresh. Tie to the demo: the config cell fixes d=1, D=1 deliberately (the walk-forward budget), and the ADF cell justifies the d. Anticipated question: "can I just always do d=1,D=1?" — for THIS traffic data, yes, and the practice does; but the ADF check is what makes that an informed choice, not a guess.

The bridge to U5 — frame it as a LIMITATION of everything built so far, not a failure. A per-series forecast is, by construction, blinkered: it sees only the target sensor's own history. The core issue Ben flagged, and the climax of the bridge section. With sparse pings (~1/min) the short-segment SKIP is the silent killer: if a vehicle traverses a short segment entirely between two consecutive pings, NO ping lands inside it, so that segment's bin gets no sample — its series is empty or holey and SARIMA can't fit. Read the figure: LEFT, two pings straddle a short segment, which is skipped; RIGHT, the mitigations. The rule of thumb to say aloud: a segment shorter than (ping cadence × typical speed) risks a skip — a bus at 30 km/h covers ~500 m in a minute, so sub-500 m segments at 1/min cadence are fragile. MITIGATIONS: make segments long enough relative to cadence; DISTRIBUTE a ping pair's speed across ALL the segments the pair crosses, weighted by length (so each crossed segment gets a sample); POOL many vehicles/days so every bin still fills; widen the time bin. With DENSE data the opposite holds — fine segments and precise traversal times are fine. Other pitfalls to name: GPS error ~10-15 m (why the ±10 m ribbon is tight), dwell (velocity 0 = STOPPED at a light, a real state, NOT missing — the practice's extension (c)), and teleport filtering (impossible jumps = bad fixes, drop them).
